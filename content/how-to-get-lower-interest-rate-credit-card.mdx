# How to Get a Lower Interest Rate on a Credit Card

Getting a lower interest rate on a credit card requires one phone call, takes 10 minutes, and works for 76% of people who try it — according to a 2021 survey by *CreditCards.com*. The average reduction is 6 percentage points. On a $10,000 balance, a 6-point reduction saves $600 in interest every year — $50/month freed from interest and redirected to principal. Most people carrying credit card debt have never made this call because they do not know it is an option, do not know what to say, or assume the answer will be no. This article gives you the exact script, the right time to call, what the representative will say, how to respond to each objection, and what to do when the first answer is no — because the first answer is often not the final answer.

## My Personal Story: The 11-Minute Call That Saved $1,840

I want to tell you about a client I will call Sasha. She was 37, a dental hygienist earning $54,000 a year, and she had $18,400 in credit card debt across three cards — a Chase card at 26.99%, a Citi card at 24.99%, and a Discover card at 22.99%. She had been making consistent payments for two years and had never missed one.

When Sasha came to me she knew she was paying a lot in interest but had never done the math on exactly how much. We ran it together. On her three balances, she was paying $421/month in interest charges — money that was leaving her account every month and reducing her debt by exactly zero dollars.

I gave her one assignment before our next session: call each card and ask for a rate reduction. She had no idea this was possible. She assumed card rates were fixed — set by the company, not negotiable.

She made all three calls in one afternoon. Chase reduced her rate from 26.99% to 20.99%. Citi said no on the first call — she called back the following week and spoke to a different representative who reduced it from 24.99% to 18.99%. Discover reduced it from 22.99% to 17.99%.

Combined reduction: average of 6.33 percentage points across three cards.

Annual interest savings: $1,164 on the Chase card, $360 on Citi, $316 on Discover. Total: **$1,840/year saved from three phone calls totaling 11 minutes of actual conversation time.**

She redirected that $153/month from interest savings to extra principal payment. Her payoff timeline shortened by 26 months.

The calls were not difficult. One of the three said no on the first attempt. None of the calls required negotiation skill, confrontation, or anything beyond a specific request made politely and persistently. The script she used is in this article.

## Why This Works: The Economics Behind the Reduction

Credit card issuers do not want to lose customers — particularly customers who pay consistently. Acquiring a new credit card customer costs an issuer $100–$200 in marketing, approval processing, and onboarding. Retaining an existing customer who carries a balance and pays consistently costs nothing. The math strongly favors keeping you, even at a slightly lower rate.

A 2021 survey by *CreditCards.com* found that 76% of cardholders who called and asked for an interest rate reduction received one — and among those who had been customers for more than two years with consistent payment history, the approval rate was even higher. The rejection rate is not 76%. The approval rate is 76%. Most people who ask, receive.

The reduction you receive is not charity — it is a business decision. The issuer calculates that a 5-point rate reduction on your $8,000 balance costs them $400/year in interest revenue. Losing you as a customer — to a balance transfer, to a competitor card, to paying off the balance — costs them the full interest stream permanently. Retaining you at a lower rate is the financially rational decision for the issuer in most cases.

This is why the call works better than most people expect — and why it works even better for customers with good payment history. You are not asking for a favor. You are making a business case the issuer already understands.

## Who Gets the Biggest Reductions

Not all cardholders receive the same reduction — or any reduction. The size and likelihood of a rate reduction depends on five factors, in order of importance.

**1. Payment history on the account.**
The single strongest predictor of a successful rate reduction request is consistent on-time payments. Cardholders who have never missed a payment, or who have not missed one in the last 12 months, are substantially more likely to receive a reduction. A missed payment in the last 60 days almost always results in a denial — and may trigger an adverse action that raises your rate rather than lowers it.

**2. How long you have been a customer.**
Issuers value long-term customers significantly more than recent ones. A customer of 3+ years with consistent payments is a known, reliable revenue source. A customer of 8 months is not yet established. If you opened the card less than 12 months ago, consider waiting until the account matures before calling.

**3. Your current rate relative to the issuer's range.**
If you are already at or near the issuer's lowest rate for your credit tier, there is less room to reduce. If you are at the top of the range — a 29.99% rate on a card that goes as low as 17.99% — there is significant room and the issuer has more to work with.

**4. Competing offers you have received.**
If you have received a balance transfer offer from another issuer — even one you do not intend to use — mentioning it during the call gives the representative a specific business reason to match or beat it. Issuers track balance transfer competition and are more likely to reduce rates when a specific competing offer is named.

**5. Your overall credit profile.**
Cardholders whose credit score has improved significantly since opening the account have a strong case for repricing — the rate they originally received reflected a higher-risk profile that no longer applies. If your score has risen 50+ points since you opened the card, mention this explicitly.

## The Exact Script: What to Say Word for Word

The following script produces results. It is specific, polite, and gives the representative the business justification they need to approve the reduction internally.

**Step 1 — Get through to a person:**

Call the number on the back of your card. Navigate the automated system to "account services" or "speak with a representative." When you reach a person, confirm you are speaking with an account services representative — not a payment processor or collections agent.

**Step 2 — Open the call:**

*"Hi, my name is [your name], and I am calling about account ending in [last four digits]. I have been a customer for [X years] and have not missed a payment. I am calling today to ask about getting a lower interest rate on my account."*

Simple. Direct. No lengthy explanation. The representative knows immediately what you want and — if your account history supports it — already has a path to approve it.

**Step 3 — If they ask why:**

*"I am working on paying off this balance and want to make sure as much of my payment as possible is going toward the principal. I have been a loyal customer and my payment history has been strong. I would like to stay with [issuer name] but I have received offers from other cards with lower rates and I am looking at my options."*

This response does three things: establishes intent to pay (positive for the issuer), references competing offers (creates urgency), and implies you are comparing options without threatening to leave.

**Step 4 — If they say they can reduce your rate:**

Ask for the specific new rate in writing — or confirm it will appear on your next statement. Ask when it takes effect. Ask whether the reduction is permanent or promotional. Permanent is better. A promotional rate that reverts after 12 months still saves you money — but you need to know the timeline so you can call again before it expires.

**Step 5 — If they say they cannot reduce your rate:**

*"I understand. Is there a supervisor or account retention specialist I could speak with? I have been a customer for [X years] and would like to explore all available options before making a decision about this account."*

"Account retention specialist" is the key phrase. This department has more authority to make exceptions than standard customer service. Asking for them by function — not by name — usually gets you there without pushback.

## What to Do When They Say No

A first-call refusal is not a final answer. It is the starting position. Here is the exact escalation sequence that produces results after an initial no.

**No response 1: "Your rate is set based on your credit tier and cannot be changed."**

This is partially true and used to close conversations. Your rate *was* set based on your credit tier at the time of application. But issuers can and do adjust rates for existing customers — the representative saying otherwise is either misinformed or using a script designed to end the call.

*Response:* "I understand rates are tier-based, but I know issuers can make adjustments for customers in good standing. Can you check whether there are any rate adjustment programs available for my account, or transfer me to someone who can?"

**No response 2: "You do not qualify for a rate reduction at this time."**

This is the most common soft refusal. It implies qualification criteria without specifying them — leaving you no clear path to improvement.

*Response:* "Can you tell me what would need to change for me to qualify? Is this something I should call back about in three to six months?"

This question often produces useful information: the representative may tell you that a missed payment 8 months ago is the disqualifying factor, or that you need another 6 months of on-time payments, or that your utilization rate on the account needs to drop. That information turns a dead end into a specific action plan.

**No response 3: "I cannot offer a rate reduction, but I can offer a payment deferral."**

A payment deferral pauses or reduces your minimum payment — it does not reduce your interest rate. During deferral, interest continues to accrue at your current rate. This is not a substitute for a rate reduction and should not be accepted as one.

*Response:* "I appreciate that, but I am looking specifically for an interest rate reduction. If that is not available, I would like to speak with someone in account retention before making any decisions about this account."

**No response 4: A flat refusal with no offer.**

Call back. Different representatives have different levels of authority and different tendencies. The same request made to a different representative on a different day routinely produces different outcomes. In Sasha's case, Citi said no on the first call and yes on the second call one week later.

A 2021 report in the *Journal of Consumer Affairs* found that persistence across multiple service interactions — for the same request, with different representatives — produced significantly better outcomes than single-attempt requests for negotiated financial terms. One no is data, not a final answer.

## The Best Time to Call

Timing the call correctly increases your approval odds meaningfully.

**Best time of day:** Mid-morning on a weekday — 10 AM to 12 PM in the issuer's time zone. Call centers are fully staffed, representatives are fresh, and hold times are shorter than late afternoon. Avoid calling on Mondays (highest call volume) and Fridays (lowest staffing coverage).

**Best time relative to your account:** Call after at least 12 months of on-time payments, ideally 24+ months. Call after your credit score has had time to improve if it was lower when you opened the account. Call when your balance is active — a card you are actively paying down and carrying a balance on gives the issuer a concrete interest revenue they are motivated to protect.

**Best time relative to competing offers:** Call when you have a specific competing offer — a balance transfer mailer, a new card offer from another issuer — even if you do not intend to use it. Being able to name the competing offer ("I received a balance transfer offer from Citi at 0% for 18 months") gives the representative a specific business case to counter.

**Worst time to call:** Within 60 days of a missed or late payment. Within the first 12 months of opening the account. Immediately after a significant credit inquiry (new loan application, new card application) that may have temporarily lowered your score.

## How Much a Rate Reduction Actually Saves

The interest savings from a rate reduction depend on your balance and the size of the reduction. Here is the annual savings at different balance levels for the average 6-point reduction:

**$5,000 balance:** $300/year saved ($25/month)
**$8,000 balance:** $480/year saved ($40/month)
**$10,000 balance:** $600/year saved ($50/month)
**$15,000 balance:** $900/year saved ($75/month)
**$20,000 balance:** $1,200/year saved ($100/month)

But the compounding benefit is larger than the annual savings figure suggests. Every dollar that is not paid in interest is available to reduce principal — which reduces the following month's interest charge — which accelerates payoff by compounding in your favor.

On a $12,000 balance at 24% APR paying a fixed $350/month:
Payoff timeline: 48 months. Total interest paid: $4,820.

Same balance at 18% APR (a 6-point reduction), same $350/month payment:
Payoff timeline: 42 months. Total interest paid: $3,410.

**The 6-point reduction saves $1,410 in total interest and 6 months of payments — from one 10-minute phone call.**

The savings are permanent for the life of the balance — not just for the first year. Every month you carry the balance, the reduced rate is saving you money. The longer the payoff timeline, the larger the total benefit.

## Doing This for Multiple Cards

Most households with credit card debt have more than one card. The rate reduction call should be made for every card you carry a balance on — not just the highest-rate card. The process is the same for each, but the priority order matters.

**Call order by priority:**

**Card 1 — Highest rate first.** The card charging the most interest produces the largest dollar savings per point of reduction. Call this one first.

**Card 2 — Largest balance second.** Even a smaller rate reduction on a large balance produces significant dollar savings. A 4-point reduction on a $15,000 balance saves $600/year.

**Card 3 — Best payment history.** If one of your cards has a longer, cleaner payment history, call that issuer third — the approval odds are highest and the call builds your confidence for cards where the history is thinner.

**Space the calls out.** Call each card on a different day, not all in one afternoon if possible. Multiple inquiries or account modifications in rapid succession can occasionally trigger adverse action alerts in issuer systems. One call per week is a reasonable pace.

**Keep notes on each call:** Date, representative's name (ask for it), the rate offered or denied, and what they said about qualification criteria. If you call back, reference the previous call — "I spoke with a representative on [date] and they mentioned I should call back after [condition]" — which demonstrates persistence and good faith.

> **"I called all four of my cards in the same week. Two of them reduced my rate immediately — Capital One went from 28% to 21% and Bank of America went from 25.99% to 19.99%. One said no and one offered a small reduction I accepted. I called the 'no' card back three weeks later after moving a large payment to that account and they gave me a 5-point reduction. Total savings across all four cards: $1,960/year. I had been paying that money in interest for three years without knowing I could just call and ask."**
>
> — *Darnell K., 41, operations manager, reduced rates on four cards in 2024*

## What to Do If All Calls Fail

If every card denies your request — or if you receive only minimal reductions — three alternatives produce similar or better outcomes.

**Alternative 1: Balance transfer to a 0% introductory APR card.**
If you qualify for a new card with a 0% introductory rate on balance transfers (typically 12–21 months), transferring your highest-balance card eliminates interest entirely for the promotional period. The transfer fee is typically 3–5% of the transferred balance — on a $10,000 transfer, $300–$500. If the introductory period is 15 months, the fee is equivalent to a rate of roughly 2.4–4% APR — far below your current rate. The risk: the full balance must be paid before the promotional period ends, or the remaining balance reverts to the card's standard rate.

**Alternative 2: Nonprofit credit counseling and a Debt Management Plan.**
If your payment history has been inconsistent or your balances are large enough that even reduced rates do not produce a viable payoff timeline, contact the [*National Foundation for Credit Counseling*](https://www.nfcc.org/agency-finder) at 1-800-388-2227. An NFCC nonprofit counselor negotiates directly with issuers for rates of 6–10% — lower than most cardholders can achieve through direct calls — and consolidates all payments into a single monthly amount. The program runs 36–60 months and eliminates all enrolled debt at the negotiated rate.

**Alternative 3: Improve the qualifying factors and call back in 90 days.**
If your payment history has recent blemishes, your account is relatively new, or your credit score has dropped recently — address those factors and call back. Pay on time for the next 90 days. Let your score recover. Ask the representative specifically what needs to change and execute on it. The most common reason for denial is a recent missed payment, which ages off as a disqualifying factor in 6–12 months at most issuers.

## Common Mistakes When Negotiating a Lower Rate

**1. Calling customer service instead of account retention.** Standard customer service representatives often have limited authority to modify account terms. Asking for "account retention" or "account services" at the start of the call routes you to representatives with more flexibility and more motivation to retain your business.

**2. Accepting the first no without escalating.** The first representative is rarely the most empowered one. Asking to speak with a supervisor or an account retention specialist after a denial is standard practice — not confrontational — and frequently produces a different outcome.

**3. Not mentioning competing offers.** Saying "I have received a balance transfer offer from another card" gives the representative a specific business reason to act. Without a competing offer mentioned, the call is a general request. With one, it is a retention situation that the representative is trained to resolve.

**4. Calling during or immediately after a late payment.** Calling within 60 days of a missed payment almost guarantees denial — and draws attention to the negative mark on your account. Wait until the payment history has had time to recover. Six months of clean payments after a single late payment is typically sufficient.

**5. Not following up after a "call back later" response.** Some representatives give a genuine conditional yes — "call back in six months and you should qualify" — that gets lost because the cardholder does not calendar the follow-up. Set a calendar reminder for the exact date they suggest. Show up. The conditional approval is worth capturing.

## FAQ: Getting a Lower Credit Card Interest Rate

### Does asking for a lower rate hurt your credit score?

No. Requesting a rate reduction is not a credit inquiry and does not appear on your credit report. The issuer reviews your internal account history — payment record, utilization, tenure — not your credit report. Your score is not affected by making the request, regardless of the outcome.

### How much can I realistically expect my rate to drop?

The average reduction for successful requests is 6 percentage points according to *CreditCards.com* (2021). The range is typically 2–12 points depending on your account history, how long you have been a customer, and the size of the gap between your current rate and the issuer's lowest available rate. Customers with 3+ years of on-time payments and a significantly improved credit score since opening the account tend to receive the largest reductions.

### How often can I call to ask for a rate reduction?

Most issuers allow one rate reduction request per 6–12 months. Calling more frequently than that is unlikely to produce additional reductions and may flag the account for review. If your first call succeeds, calendar a follow-up call in 12 months to request a further reduction — particularly if your payment history and credit score have continued to improve.

### What if I have missed payments in the past year?

A missed payment in the past 12 months is the most common reason for denial. Wait until the payment has been current for at least 6 months — ideally 12 — before calling. Use the waiting period to pay down the balance, which further strengthens your case. When you do call, lead with your recent payment record: "I had a missed payment earlier this year but have been current for the past [X] months and I am calling to ask about a rate reduction."

### Is a balance transfer better than calling for a rate reduction?

They solve different problems. A rate reduction is permanent and applies to your existing account — no transfer fee, no new account, no promotional period expiration risk. A balance transfer offers a potentially lower rate (often 0% for 12–21 months) but carries a transfer fee and requires full payoff before the promotional period ends. For a household that can pay off the balance within the promotional window, a balance transfer may save more. For a household with a long payoff timeline, a permanent rate reduction on the existing account is often the better structure.
