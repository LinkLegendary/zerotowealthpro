# What Order Should I Pay Off My Debts?

The order you pay off your debts matters more than almost any other decision in a debt payoff plan — and the answer is not simply "highest interest rate first." The correct payoff sequence depends on debt type, legal consequences of non-payment, secured versus unsecured status, and whether any accounts are approaching a statute of limitations that changes your legal exposure. Paying $200 extra per month toward a 6.8% federal student loan while carrying a $9,000 credit card at 24% APR costs approximately $1,600 in unnecessary interest every year. Getting the sequence wrong is expensive. Getting it right — particularly when you have multiple debt types — is the single highest-leverage financial decision available to most households carrying mixed debt. This article gives you the exact priority framework, the math behind each decision, and the answers to the most common sequencing questions.

## My Personal Story: The Client Paying Off the Wrong Debt for Three Years

I want to tell you about a client I will call Marlene. She was 41, a dental office manager earning $52,000 a year, and she had five debts when she came to me:

- Credit card A: $6,200 at 26.99% APR
- Credit card B: $3,800 at 22.99% APR
- Car loan: $11,400 at 6.9% APR
- Federal student loan: $18,200 at 5.05% APR
- Medical debt: $1,400 in collections

For three years Marlene had been making $300/month in extra principal payments on her car loan — because it was the largest single monthly payment and eliminating it felt like the most meaningful financial relief she could get. Her logic was understandable. Her math was significantly wrong.

During those three years her car loan balance fell from $14,200 to $11,400 — a $2,800 reduction. Her credit card balances, which she was paying minimum payments on, had fallen from a combined $10,600 to $10,000 — a $600 reduction despite paying $7,200 in minimum payments over 36 months. Of that $7,200 in minimum payments, $5,840 had gone to interest. Her $600 balance reduction on $7,200 paid represented an 8% efficiency rate.

Meanwhile, the $18,900 in interest she paid on the car loan and student loan during those three years — at 6.9% and 5.05% respectively — was real but manageable. The $5,840 in credit card interest was the expensive part — and it was the part that received only the minimum.

When we restructured her payoff sequence — minimum payments on the car and student loan, $300/month extra to the highest-rate credit card — she eliminated both cards in 23 months and saved $4,200 in total interest compared to continuing her original approach. Her car loan and student loan extended by 7 months — which cost $340 in additional interest at the lower rates. Net benefit of the sequence change: $3,860, from changing the order of payments without changing the total amount paid.

The sequence is the leverage point.

## The Priority Framework: Five Tiers in Order

Debt payoff order is determined by five factors in a specific hierarchy. Work through this framework for every debt on your list.

**Tier 1 — Debts that threaten shelter, transportation, or income (pay these first)**
**Tier 2 — High-rate unsecured debt (attack after Tier 1 minimums are covered)**
**Tier 3 — Collections approaching statute of limitations (handle strategically)**
**Tier 4 — Mid-rate secured and unsecured debt (minimum payments while attacking Tier 2)**
**Tier 5 — Low-rate debt with tax advantages (minimum payments only — possibly forever)**

This is not the same as "pay the highest APR first." Tier 1 includes debts at 0–7% APR that must be paid ahead of a 26% credit card because non-payment triggers consequences — repossession, eviction, wage garnishment — that are more damaging than interest rate differences. The framework accounts for both financial and legal risk simultaneously.

## Tier 1: Debts That Threaten Shelter, Transportation, or Income

These debts get paid before any extra payment goes anywhere — regardless of their interest rate.

**Mortgage or rent:** Non-payment triggers eviction or foreclosure proceedings that are expensive, credit-damaging, and can take months to resolve — months during which you are paying the same housing cost plus legal fees plus potentially a security deposit on new housing. A 4.5% mortgage payment is Tier 1. A 26% credit card is Tier 2. The mortgage gets paid first every time.

**Car payment (if the car is required for work):** If losing the car means losing income — you drive to clients, you work a route, you live somewhere without public transit — car repossession is a Tier 1 consequence. A 6.9% car payment required for income generation takes priority over a credit card minimum payment.

**Utilities essential for habitation:** Heat, electricity, and water shutoffs cascade into food spoilage, health consequences, and habitability issues. Utility payments required to maintain a livable home are Tier 1 regardless of rate.

**IRS tax debt:** The IRS has collection powers that no private creditor has — wage garnishment without a court judgment, bank account levies, tax refund seizure, federal liens on property. An IRS payment plan or installment agreement is Tier 1. Ignoring IRS debt while paying credit card minimums is the sequencing error with the most severe long-term consequences.

**What Tier 1 means in practice:** Make the full required payment on every Tier 1 debt every month, before calculating what is available for extra payments elsewhere. Missing a Tier 1 payment to make an extra credit card payment is never the right sequence.

## Tier 2: High-Rate Unsecured Debt — Your Primary Attack Target

After Tier 1 obligations are fully covered, every available extra dollar goes to the highest-rate unsecured debt. This tier includes credit cards, personal loans above approximately 12% APR, payday loans, and any other unsecured debt charging a rate that makes carrying the balance genuinely expensive.

**Why Tier 2 gets all extra payments:**

Unsecured debt at 20–30% APR is the most expensive debt most households carry. It is also the most legally forgiving to miss — non-payment of unsecured debt does not trigger repossession or eviction. It damages credit and eventually leads to collections and possible lawsuits — but those consequences unfold slowly enough that the high-rate payoff can be aggressive without Tier 1 risk.

The math on prioritizing Tier 2 is unambiguous. On a $10,000 credit card at 24% APR:

Monthly interest charge: $200
Extra $300/month applied here → card paid off in 27 months, total interest $2,400
Same $300/month applied to a 6% car loan instead → card takes 104 months at minimum, total interest $12,800+

The difference in total interest paid between applying $300/month to the credit card versus the car loan: **$10,400 on a $10,000 balance.** Sequence matters more than payment size.

**Within Tier 2 — avalanche or snowball:**

Once you have identified all Tier 2 debts, the sub-order question is avalanche (highest APR first) versus snowball (smallest balance first).

**Avalanche:** Mathematically optimal. On a typical two-card load of $6,000 at 24% and $4,000 at 20%, avalanche saves $400–$800 in total interest versus snowball.

**Snowball:** Psychologically optimal for households that have previously abandoned a debt payoff plan. The first account elimination — regardless of rate — produces a concrete win that sustains 24–48 months of commitment. A 2016 study in the *Journal of Consumer Research* found snowball users more likely to eliminate their total debt load precisely because early wins sustained motivation.

**The honest recommendation:** If you have successfully maintained a budget for 12+ months without quitting, use the avalanche. If you have quit a debt payoff plan before, use the snowball for Tier 2. Both are dramatically better than the alternative — sending extra payments to low-rate Tier 4 or Tier 5 debt while Tier 2 compounds at 24%.

## Tier 3: Collections — Handle Strategically, Not Automatically

Collections are the most misunderstood category in debt payoff sequencing. The instinct is to pay them off as quickly as possible to clean up the credit report. The strategic reality is more nuanced — and paying a collection at the wrong time or in the wrong way can produce worse outcomes than the alternatives.

**The statute of limitations question:**

Every state has a statute of limitations on debt — the period during which a creditor or collection agency can sue you to collect. After the statute of limitations expires, the debt is "time-barred" — still legally owed, still reportable on your credit report for up to 7 years from original delinquency, but no longer collectible through a lawsuit. Most states set this at 3–6 years for credit card debt.

A collection that is 5 years old in a state with a 6-year statute of limitations has one year of lawsuit risk remaining — and 2 years until it falls off your credit report automatically. Paying it now satisfies the debt but does not remove the collection notation (unless you negotiate pay-for-delete). Ignoring it for 12 more months eliminates the lawsuit risk entirely, and it falls off your report in 24 months regardless.

**The strategic framework for collections:**

**If the collection is less than 2 years old:** Dispute for errors first (Step 1 in any credit rebuilding plan). If accurate and no errors, negotiate pay-for-delete before paying — get the agreement in writing before sending a dollar.

**If the collection is 2–5 years old:** Consider the statute of limitations in your state. If lawsuit risk is still active and the balance is significant (over $1,500), paying or settling is worth considering. If the balance is small or the statute is close to expiring, weigh the cost of payment against the benefit of natural aging.

**If the collection is 5+ years old:** In most states, the debt is approaching the statute of limitations expiration and within 2 years of falling off your credit report. Paying it now satisfies the legal obligation but does not remove the credit report notation. Waiting for natural aging is often the most rational financial decision.

**Warning — do not restart the clock:** Making a payment on a time-barred debt or acknowledging the debt in writing can restart the statute of limitations in some states — resurrecting lawsuit risk on a debt that had expired. Verify your state's rules before making any payment or written acknowledgment on old collections.

**Marlene's $1,400 medical collection:** We checked — it was 3 years old in a state with a 4-year statute. One year of lawsuit risk remaining, 4 years until natural removal. We negotiated a pay-for-delete for $840 (60 cents on the dollar), received the agreement in writing, paid it, and it was removed from all three bureaus within 30 days. Score improvement: 41 points. That was the right move at that age and balance. At 5 years old in the same state, we would have waited.

## Tier 4: Mid-Rate Secured and Unsecured Debt

This tier includes car loans (typically 5–9%), personal loans at 8–15%, and any secured debt not in Tier 1. These debts get minimum payments only while Tier 2 is being eliminated.

**The math justification:**

Paying extra on a 7% car loan while carrying a 24% credit card costs you the 17-point interest rate differential on every extra dollar directed to the car. That differential compounds over the full payoff timeline. On $5,000 in extra principal payments over 3 years, directing them to a 7% car loan versus a 24% credit card costs approximately $2,550 in unnecessary interest.

**The exception — secured debt at risk:**

If a Tier 4 secured debt is delinquent and the asset is at risk of repossession, it temporarily becomes Tier 1. A car loan that is 60 days past due and facing repossession takes priority over a current credit card, because repossession eliminates transportation-to-income and generates repossession fees and deficiency balance claims that cost more than the interest rate differential.

**After Tier 2 is eliminated:**

Once all high-rate unsecured debt is gone, extra payments previously directed to Tier 2 roll to Tier 4 — specifically the highest-rate debt within this tier. This is the rollover principle: freed payment from eliminated Tier 2 accounts becomes the attack payment for Tier 4. A $400/month payment that eliminated a credit card now attacks the car loan, accelerating payoff without any increase in total payment.

## Tier 5: Low-Rate Debt With Tax Advantages — Minimum Payments Only

This tier includes federal student loans (typically 4–7%), mortgages (typically 3–7%), and any debt where the interest rate is low enough — and the tax deductibility significant enough — that extra payments produce marginal financial benefit compared to investing the same dollars.

**Federal student loans:**

Federal student loans at 5–7% APR come with income-driven repayment options, forgiveness programs, deferment during hardship, and death/disability discharge provisions that private debt does not carry. The effective after-tax cost is further reduced if you itemize the student loan interest deduction (up to $2,500/year for qualifying borrowers).

Paying extra on a 5.05% federal student loan while carrying a 24% credit card costs you 19 percentage points per dollar. After the credit card is gone, the student loan remains a reasonable candidate for extra payments — but not before.

**Mortgages:**

A 4–6% mortgage has an after-tax cost of 3–4.5% for itemizing homeowners. A dollar of extra mortgage payment produces a guaranteed 3–4.5% return. A dollar invested in a diversified stock portfolio has historically produced 7–10% annually. For most households with long time horizons, investing the extra dollar rather than paying the mortgage ahead produces better long-term outcomes — though the mortgage payoff provides the psychological benefit of reduced fixed obligations.

**The practical Tier 5 rule:** Make every required payment on time. Direct zero extra dollars to Tier 5 while any Tier 2 debt exists. After Tier 2 and Tier 4 are eliminated, revisit whether extra mortgage and student loan payments make more sense than investing — the answer depends on your interest rates, tax situation, and investment time horizon.

## The Mixed-Debt Payoff Decision Tree

Use this framework every time you have extra money available to apply to debt.

**Question 1: Are all Tier 1 payments current?**
No → Pay the Tier 1 arrears first. No exceptions.
Yes → proceed to Question 2.

**Question 2: Do you have any Tier 2 debt (unsecured debt above 12% APR)?**
Yes → Every extra dollar goes to the highest-rate Tier 2 debt (avalanche) or lowest-balance Tier 2 debt (snowball).
No → proceed to Question 3.

**Question 3: Do you have any Tier 3 collections within the statute of limitations?**
Yes → Evaluate age, balance, and lawsuit risk. Negotiate pay-for-delete if paying.
No → proceed to Question 4.

**Question 4: Do you have Tier 4 debt (mid-rate secured/unsecured)?**
Yes → Roll the freed payment from eliminated Tier 2 accounts to the highest-rate Tier 4 debt.
No → proceed to Question 5.

**Question 5: Only Tier 5 debt remains (mortgage, federal student loans).**
Compare after-tax cost of extra debt payment to expected investment returns. For most households with 10+ year time horizons, investing beats extra mortgage payment. For households near retirement, the guaranteed return of mortgage payoff may be preferable.

## What to Do When You Cannot Cover All Minimums

If total minimum payments across all debt tiers exceed your available income, the sequence changes — from optimization to triage.

**Triage sequence:**

**1. Shelter first.** Rent or mortgage payment. Non-negotiable.
**2. Essential utilities.** Heat, electricity, water.
**3. Transportation to income.** Car payment and insurance if the car is required for work.
**4. Food.** Grocery-level — not restaurants.
**5. IRS installment agreement.** If active, honor it.
**6. Unsecured minimum payments.** In order of which creditor is most likely to sue first — typically accounts that are already delinquent or in collections.

When income does not cover all minimums, contact an NFCC nonprofit counselor immediately. A Debt Management Plan can reduce interest rates to 6–10% across all enrolled accounts, making minimum payments manageable on income that cannot currently support them. Contact the [*National Foundation for Credit Counseling*](https://www.nfcc.org/agency-finder) at 1-800-388-2227.

## Common Sequencing Mistakes

**1. Paying extra on the car loan before eliminating credit cards.** The most common sequencing error — driven by the psychological weight of a large monthly car payment. The math consistently favors attacking high-rate unsecured debt first. Marlene's three years of extra car payments cost her $3,860 in unnecessary interest compared to the optimal sequence.

**2. Paying any collection without first negotiating pay-for-delete.** A paid collection remains on your credit report for 7 years. If you are going to pay it, the only rational approach is to attempt pay-for-delete first — get the agreement in writing before sending a dollar.

**3. Ignoring IRS debt to pay credit cards.** The IRS has collection powers — wage garnishment, bank levies, property liens — that credit card issuers do not have without a court judgment. An IRS balance in active collection is always Tier 1 regardless of the credit card rates.

**4. Making extra student loan payments while carrying credit card debt.** Federal student loans at 5–7% are the last debt that should receive extra payments while 20–27% credit card balances exist. Every extra dollar to the student loan while carrying a credit card costs approximately 15–20 percentage points of unnecessary interest.

**5. Paying off a credit card and then closing the account.** Closing a paid credit card reduces your total available credit — which increases utilization on remaining cards — and eventually shortens your average account age. Keep paid accounts open and active. The elimination of the balance is the goal, not the elimination of the account.

## FAQ: What Order Should I Pay Off My Debts?

### Should I pay off credit cards or student loans first?

Credit cards first — almost without exception. A credit card at 20–27% APR is typically 15–20 percentage points more expensive than a federal student loan at 5–7%. Every extra dollar sent to a student loan while a credit card balance exists costs that 15–20 point differential in unnecessary interest. Federal student loans also carry income-driven repayment, forgiveness programs, and deferment options that justify carrying them longer than high-rate unsecured debt.

### Should I pay off credit cards or my car loan first?

Credit cards first — unless the car loan is delinquent and facing repossession. A current car loan at 6–9% is dramatically cheaper than a credit card at 20–27%. Make the minimum car payment every month and direct all extra payment to the highest-rate credit card. Once all credit cards are eliminated, roll the freed payment to the car loan.

### What if I have medical debt?

Medical debt is typically unsecured, often interest-free or low-interest, and medical debt under $500 was removed from credit reports by the major bureaus in 2023. Medical debt under $500 can be deprioritized — pay it when you have capacity, after Tier 2 is eliminated. Medical debt above $500, particularly if it has gone to collections, should be evaluated using the Tier 3 collections framework: check the age, negotiate pay-for-delete, and factor in the statute of limitations for your state.

### Is it better to pay off one debt completely or reduce all balances?

Pay off one debt completely — the highest-rate one — before reducing other balances. Spreading extra payments across all accounts reduces each balance slightly but eliminates nothing and frees no minimum payment for rollover. The first account elimination frees a minimum payment that rolls to the next account, compounding payoff speed. This is the rollover principle — and it is more powerful than any alternative allocation approach.

### What if a debt collector is threatening to sue?

A lawsuit threat escalates a collection from Tier 3 to Tier 1 treatment — not because the debt becomes more expensive, but because a judgment gives the creditor wage garnishment power that changes your financial situation fundamentally. Contact an NFCC counselor or consumer law attorney immediately. Many states provide free legal aid for debt collection lawsuits. Do not ignore a lawsuit summons — a default judgment is entered automatically if you do not respond within the required window (typically 20–30 days depending on state).
